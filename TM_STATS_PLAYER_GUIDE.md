## Logging Your First Round

After every round, take 5 minutes to log it. The more rounds you log, the more useful TM Stats becomes — patterns emerge over time.

### Step 1: Start a new round

From any screen, tap the **+** button or go to **Rounds → New round**.

### Step 2: Set up the round

Fill in the basics:

| Field | What to enter |
|---|---|
| Course name | Where you played — e.g., "Royal Lytham" |
| Date | When you played (defaults to today) |
| Holes | 9 or 18 |
| Round type | Practice, Competition, or Tournament |
| Input mode | **Quick entry** to start (see below) |

### Step 3: Enter hole by hole

For each hole, enter:
- **Par** — is it a par 3, 4, or 5?
- **Score** — how many shots did you take?
- **Fairway hit?** — did your tee shot land in the fairway? (Par 4s and 5s only)
- **Green in regulation?** — did you reach the green in the expected number of shots?
- **Putts** — how many putts did you take once on the green?

**Don't worry about getting it perfect.** If you can't remember whether you hit the fairway on hole 7, tap "no" and move on. Approximate data is better than no data.

### Step 4: Add notes (optional)

At the bottom of the round entry screen, there is a notes box. Use it for anything you want to remember:
- *"Played well front nine, fell apart on the back after the double on 13"*
- *"Driving was excellent today — new grip is working"*
- *"Windy and cold — not representative of my normal game"*

These notes feed into your AI coaching feedback, making it more specific and relevant.

### Step 5: Save

Tap **Save round**. You are done.

## Reading Your Stats

After logging a few rounds, head to the **Stats** page (bottom navigation).

### What you will see

**Average score**  
Your average score relative to par across all your rounds. If this is +12, you are averaging 12 over par per round.

**Fairways in Regulation (FIR%)**  
What percentage of par 4 and par 5 tee shots landed in the fairway. Higher is better — it means you are starting holes in good positions.

**Greens in Regulation (GIR%)**  
What percentage of holes you reached the green in the regulation number of shots (1 on par 3s, 2 on par 4s, 3 on par 5s). This is one of the strongest predictors of scoring — the more greens you hit, the lower your score.

**Putts per hole**  
How many putts you average per hole. Tour professionals average around 1.7. Club golfers typically average 1.9 to 2.2.

**Up & Down %**  
When you miss the green, how often do you chip on and make the putt. The better this number, the more damage you can limit from missed greens.

### Scoring distribution

The coloured chart on the Stats page shows what percentage of your holes are eagles, birdies, pars, bogeys, doubles, and worse. Most improvement for amateur golfers comes from **reducing double bogeys and worse**, not adding more birdies.

## Full Tracking Mode

Quick entry is fine for logging scores. Full tracking is where TM Stats gets powerful.

In Full Tracking mode, you record every shot — the distance to the pin and the type of lie you were in. It takes an extra 2–3 minutes per round and unlocks **Strokes Gained analysis** — the most powerful performance tool in golf.

### How to use Full Tracking

When setting up a new round, select **Full Tracking** as the input mode instead of Quick Entry.

For each shot on each hole, record:
- **Distance to pin** — in yards (roughly is fine — within 5 yards is accurate enough)
- **Lie type** — select from: tee, fairway, rough, bunker, recovery, green

That's it. The system handles everything else automatically.

**What gets auto-calculated for you:**
- Whether you hit the fairway (FIR)
- Whether you hit the green in regulation (GIR)
- How many putts you took
- Whether you made up and down from off the green
- Whether you made a sand save from a bunker

You never manually enter yes/no for any of these — the system works it out from your shot data.

### Tips for full tracking on the course

- **Enter shots as you walk, not all at once.** After each shot, take 5 seconds to log distance and lie.
- **Pace off yardages or use your rangefinder.** Exact distances give exact Strokes Gained numbers.
- **Don't overthink lie types.** Fairway or rough is usually obvious. Use "recovery" for any shot where your main goal is just getting back to a playable position.
- **On the green, every shot is "green" lie type.** Including short putts.

## Strokes Gained — What It Means

Strokes Gained (SG) is the number that tells you *where* you are winning and losing shots against other golfers of your ability level.

### The simple explanation

Every shot in golf can be rated. Before you play a shot, there is an expected number of shots a player of your ability should take to hole out from that position. After you play the shot, that expected number changes.

**Strokes Gained on a shot = (expected before) − (expected after) − 1**

If you gained ground compared to the expectation, your SG for that shot is positive. If you lost ground, it is negative.

**Example:**  
You are a 15-handicap. You have a 150-yard approach to a par 4. From that position, the benchmark says a 15-handicap player will take about 2.9 more shots to hole out.

You hit the shot to 10 feet. From 10 feet, the benchmark says you will take about 1.5 more shots.

**SG = 2.9 − 1.5 − 1 = +0.4**  
You gained 0.4 shots on that approach — you outperformed the benchmark for your ability level.

### The four categories

SG is split into four areas:

**SG: Off Tee**  
All tee shots on par 4s and par 5s. Measures how well your tee ball sets up the rest of the hole.

**SG: Approach**  
Shots from outside 30 yards that are not tee shots. Irons, hybrids, and long approach shots.

**SG: Around Green**  
Shots from within 30 yards when you are not on the putting surface. Chipping, pitching, and bunker play.

**SG: Putting**  
Every stroke taken on the putting surface.

### Setting Your SG Baseline

By default, TM Stats sets your comparison baseline automatically from your handicap. You can override this at any time in **Profile → Strokes Gained baseline**.

| Baseline | Who should choose this |
|---|---|
| **PGA Tour** | Plus-handicap players, elite amateurs, county / national level, mini-tour |
| **Scratch** | Single-figure players at the top of the amateur game |
| **Low amateur (1–9)** | Competitive club golfers |
| **Mid amateur (10–18)** | Typical club member |
| **High amateur (19–28)** | Developing players |
| **High handicapper (29–36)** | Recreational |
| **Beginner (37+)** | New to the game |

**If you are a plus-handicap or elite amateur player:** Set your baseline to **PGA Tour**. Your SG numbers will show negative values — that is completely normal and expected. What matters is the *pattern*: which categories are closest to Tour standard and which have the biggest gap. A −1.4 in putting and −2.8 in approach tells you exactly where the difference between your game and Tour level lives.

Tap any baseline to select it. Tap the same one again to reset to automatic.

### How to read your SG numbers

| Value | What it means |
|---|---|
| +0.5 or better | Significantly above benchmark — a genuine strength |
| +0.1 to +0.5 | Above benchmark — performing well in this area |
| −0.1 to +0.1 | At benchmark — neither gaining nor losing shots |
| −0.1 to −0.5 | Below benchmark — room to improve |
| −0.5 or worse | Significantly below benchmark — priority area |

**Your SG numbers are compared to players of YOUR handicap** — not Tour professionals. A 20-handicap with SG: Off Tee of +0.3 is hitting their tee shots better than the average 20-handicap. That is a real achievement.

## AI Coaching

Pro plan users receive personalised AI coaching feedback on every round and across their overall stats.

### Choosing your coach persona

In **Profile → Settings**, you choose your coaching style. This changes how all AI feedback is delivered to you.

| Persona | Good for |
|---|---|
| **Club Pro** | Most players. Friendly, practical, no jargon. |
| **Fundamentals Coach** | Players who want basics-first, direct instruction. |
| **Technical Analyst** | Players who want to understand the mechanics behind every observation. |
| **Short Game Specialist** | Players whose biggest weakness is inside 100 yards. |
| **Ball Flight Coach** | Players who want to understand their shot shape and fix dispersion. |
| **Encourager** | Players who need confidence and positive reinforcement. |
| **Straight Talker** | Players who want blunt, direct, no-nonsense feedback. |

**You can change this at any time.** Try a few and see which gives you feedback that actually resonates and makes you want to act on it.

### Choosing your feedback level

Also in **Profile → Settings**, you choose how detailed the coaching should be.

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| **Simple** | 2–3 key points in plain English. 2 drills. |
| **Intermediate** | Stats context, 3 observations, 3 drills with instructions. |
| **Advanced** | Root cause analysis, patterns, 3 drills with full rationale. |

### Round coaching

After saving a round, go to the round's detail page and tap **Get coaching feedback**. The AI analyses:
- Your score relative to par
- SG across all 4 categories (full tracking rounds)
- GIR, putts, up & down, sand saves
- Your round conditions and notes
- Your coach persona and feedback level

It returns coaching feedback specific to that round, with named practice drills you can do at your next range session.

**Example output (Club Pro, Intermediate level):**

> *"A solid ball striking day, Rob — your approach play was excellent and your GIR% of 72% would be the envy of most club golfers. The round was let down by the flat stick. Averaging 2.3 putts per hole is costing you around 4–5 shots every time you play. The good news is this is the most improvable area in your game right now.*
>
> *DRILL 1: Gate drill — Place two tees just wider than your putter head, 3 feet from the hole. Hit 50 putts through the gate. No lip-outs allowed.*
>
> *DRILL 2: Distance control ladder — Place balls at 20, 30, 40 feet. Hit 3 putts from each distance, focusing on getting the ball to finish 1 foot past the hole.*
>
> *DRILL 3: Routine drill — Before every putt in practice, stand behind the ball for 5 seconds, visualise the line, then step in and putt within 10 seconds. Build a consistent process.*"

### Round review

The **Round Review** feature gives you a structured, three-part assessment of a round:
- **WEAKNESS:** The single biggest issue in that round
- **FIX:** The single most impactful thing to work on
- **MODE:** A recommended coaching style for your game, based on the data

Use this when you want a quick, clear read of where a round went wrong and what to address first.

### Practice plan

The **Practice Plan** feature generates a structured 45-minute practice session based on your round's weakest areas. Four timed blocks, with specific drills for each.

## SG by Distance Band

On the round detail page, scroll to the **Advanced Performance Breakdown** section (tap to expand). This shows your SG broken down into specific distance ranges:

| Band | Typical shots |
|---|---|
| 0–30 yards | Chips, pitches, short bunker shots |
| 30–75 yards | Wedge approaches |
| 75–125 yards | Short irons |
| 125–175 yards | Mid irons |
| 175+ yards | Long irons, hybrids, woods |

**Why this matters:**  
A player losing shots on approach overall might be losing them specifically from 125–175 yards (mid iron problem) while being perfectly solid from other distances. Or they might be strong with short irons but struggle with 175+ yard approach shots.

Distance band SG takes you from knowing *what* category is weak to knowing *exactly which shots* within that category to work on. This is tour-level precision applied to your game.

**Example:**  
SG: Approach shows −0.6 overall for a round. Opening the distance bands reveals:
- 75–125 yards: +0.2 (solid with short irons)
- 125–175 yards: −0.9 (losing almost a full shot per round with mid irons)
- 175+: +0.1 (comfortable with long irons)

The practice prescription is specific: mid iron work from 125–175 yards. Not generic iron practice — a precise target.

## Benchmark Comparison (Stats Page)

On the Stats page, your data is shown alongside the **benchmark average** for your handicap band.

This answers the question: "Am I genuinely weak in this area, or does everyone at my level struggle here?"

**Example:**  
Your sand save % is 28%. Is that poor? If the benchmark for your handicap is 24%, you are actually above average from bunkers — not a priority. If the benchmark is 35%, you have a real gap to close.

Without the benchmark, you might work on something that isn't actually below average. With it, you know exactly where you stand against your peers.

## Score Impact Simulator (Stats Page)

One of the most motivating tools on the platform. The Score Impact Simulator answers: **"If I improve this area, what would it actually do to my average score?"**

Adjust the SG slider for any category and see your projected average score move in real time.

**Example:**  
Your SG: Putting is currently averaging −0.8 per round. Moving it to just −0.3 (still below benchmark, just less so) shows a projected score improvement of 3 shots per round on average.

This gives your practice a genuine purpose. You are not just hitting balls — you are working on something with a quantifiable impact on your scores.

## Golf DNA (Profile Page)

After logging 2 or more full-tracking rounds, your Golf DNA card appears in your Profile. It summarises your game in two data points, updated automatically as you log more rounds.

**Strength** — your best SG category on average across your last 10 full-tracking rounds  
**Opportunity** — your weakest SG category — the area with the most room to improve

**Example:**  
> Strength: **Driving** (+0.34 SG avg)  
> Opportunity: **Putting** (−0.82 SG avg)

Golf DNA updates silently in the background. Check it every few weeks — as your game changes, your strength and opportunity areas will shift.

## Mental Game (/mental)

Available to Pro plan users. A free-form space to talk through the mental side of your game with an AI golf mentor.

Unlike the structured coaching features, the Mental Game page is conversational. You type whatever is on your mind — a recurring issue, a specific fear, a pattern of performance under pressure — and receive a calm, experienced response focused on what to do on the course.

**This is not therapy.** It is practical golf mentoring: perspective, simple cues, and next steps on the course.

**How to use it well:**  
Be specific and honest. "I get nervous" is hard to help with. "I three-putt every time I have a birdie putt in a competition round because I decelerate through the ball" gives the system something concrete to work with.

## Getting the Most from Your Coach

If you are part of a coach's squad on TM Stats, your coach can see your stats, your round history, and your SG breakdown.

**To maximise this:**

- **Log rounds consistently.** One round a month isn't enough data for meaningful coaching. Even if your coach doesn't mention specific rounds in your lesson, the data builds a picture over time.

- **Use full tracking mode.** Quick entry gives scores. Full tracking gives your coach the Strokes Gained breakdown they need to give you specific, targeted coaching rather than general advice.

- **Add notes to your rounds.** The notes field is visible to your coach. If something specific happened in a round — a mental wobble, an injury niggle, an intentional drill you were running — write it down. Your coach will see it.

- **Check your round detail page after lessons.** If your coach has used the AI Challenge to enhance your coaching feedback, you will see it labelled "From your coach" on any challenged rounds. This is your coach's view of what the data means in the context of your programme.

## Tips for Serious Improvement

**1. Log every round, not just the good ones.**  
The analysis only gets accurate over a sample of rounds. One exceptional round and one disaster will tell you nothing meaningful. Ten rounds tell you your real game.

**2. Use full tracking mode for at least half your rounds.**  
Quick entry is fine for keeping a record. Full tracking is what unlocks the insight. If you only full-track your range sessions or competition rounds, that's still valuable data.

**3. Look at trends, not individual rounds.**  
A single round where your putting was poor means nothing. SG: Putting averaging −0.7 over your last 8 rounds means a lot. Always use the Stats page with a filter of at least 5 rounds.

**4. Act on the data.**  
The platform tells you exactly what to work on and gives you drills. The only variable is whether you take it to the range. A 15-minute focused session on your weakest SG category is worth more than an hour of hitting balls at nothing in particular.

**5. Update your handicap when it changes — and set your baseline correctly.**  
Your handicap setting affects your SG baseline and all coaching context. Keep it current. If you are a plus-handicap player or competing at elite level, go to **Profile → Strokes Gained baseline** and select **PGA Tour** — this gives you the most meaningful comparison for where your game actually sits.
